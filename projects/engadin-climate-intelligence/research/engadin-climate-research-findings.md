# Engadin Climate & Environmental Research Brief (2026–2036)
# Klima- und Umweltforschungsbericht Engadin (2026–2036)

---

## 1. Executive Summary / Zusammenfassung

### English

This research brief presents a comprehensive assessment of climate and environmental conditions and projections for the Engadin region (Upper Engadin, Lower Engadin, Val Müstair) over the 2026–2036 period. Key findings:

- **Temperature**: Switzerland's mean temperature is already +3.0°C above the pre-industrial baseline (1871–1900) as of 2026. By 2035, projections indicate +0.6 to +1.9°C additional warming above the 1981–2010 baseline (depending on emission scenario). Alpine regions experience more pronounced summer warming than lowland areas.
- **Precipitation**: Winter precipitation is increasing but shifting from snow to rain at altitudes below ~1,500 m. Summer drought risk is rising. Extreme precipitation events are becoming more frequent and intense, with a 2°C warming doubling the probability of extreme Alpine rainfall.
- **Snow cover**: Snow cover duration has shortened by ~8.9 days per decade across all Swiss Alpine stations. Under high-emission scenarios, snowpack could shrink up to 70% by end-of-century. Only areas above ~2,500 m will retain reliable natural snow cover.
- **Water resources**: Alpine river discharge patterns are shifting from summer-dominant to more evenly distributed flow, with summer low flows decreasing by up to 40%. Engadin's three major glaciers (Morteratsch, Palü, Roseg) are retreating rapidly — Morteratsch alone loses ~50 m/year frontally and 6% of volume in record years.
- **Permafrost**: Ground temperatures are rising at +0.02°C/year at depth across Swiss permafrost sites. Rock glacier velocities have accelerated markedly since 2013. Over one-third of 152 Swiss Alpine Club huts are endangered by thawing permafrost.
- **Extreme events**: Heat stress is the greatest climate risk for Switzerland (FOEN 2025). Flood, drought, and intense precipitation risks are all increasing, with cantons required to develop strategic risk plans by 2031.

**EN**: The Engadin is a characteristic dry inner-Alpine valley with the highest relative sunshine duration in Switzerland. The zero-degree line has risen more than 100 m per decade in spring and summer over the past five decades. Für das Engadin (Talboden auf ~1.700–1.800 m) bedeutet dies, dass zuverlässiger Schneefall zunehmend auf Dezember–Februar beschränkt ist, wobei die Übergangsjahreszeiten regenbestimmt werden.

### 2.4 Water Resources / Wasserressourcen

#### Inn River Flow Projections / Inn-Abflussprojektionen

**EN**: The Inn river, which drains the entire Engadin, will experience a fundamental shift in its seasonal flow pattern during 2026–2036. Currently a glacier/snowmelt-dominated regime with peak summer discharge, the Inn is transitioning to a more rain-dominated regime. Summer flows will decrease as glacier melt contribution diminishes and spring snowmelt occurs earlier. Winter flows will increase due to more precipitation falling as rain. This has significant implications for hydropower (major installations at S-chanf, Ova Spin, and Punt dal Gall), agriculture, and downstream water availability.

**DE**: Der Inn, der das gesamte Engadin entwässert, wird im Zeitraum 2026–2036 einen grundlegenden Wandel seines saisonalen Abflussmusters erleben. Derzeit ein gletscher-/schneeschmelzedominiertes Regime mit sommerlichem Spitzenabfluss, wandelt sich der Inn zu einem mehr regendominierten Regime. Sommerabflüsse werden abnehmen, da der Gletscherschmelzbeitrag sinkt und die Schneeschmelze früher einsetzt. Winterabflüsse werden zunehmen, da mehr Niederschlag als Regen fällt.

**EN**: For the 2026–2036 period, all three Engadin glaciers will continue rapid retreat. Morteratsch, the most monitored, will likely lose another 20–30% of its current volume by 2036 based on recent loss rates (4–6%/year in extreme years, ~2.5%/year average). Peak water — the point of maximum glacier-fed river discharge — is likely within or just past the 2026–2036 window for the Bernina group, after which glacier contribution to Inn river flow will decline permanently. Palü and Roseg glaciers, being smaller, face even more proportional losses.

**EN**: The Upper Engadin is a key PERMOS monitoring region, with the longest continuous permafrost temperature record in Switzerland on Piz Corvatsch (since 1987). Infrastructure at high altitudes (cable cars, mountain huts, roads) faces increasing geotechnical risk. **EN**: The Engadin faces a specific mix of extreme event risks:
1. **Heat stress**: While less severe than lowland cities, record temperatures are increasingly common at altitude. The Engadin's traditionally cool summers are becoming warmer.
2. **Drought**: As an already dry inner-Alpine valley (700–1,000 mm/year), the Engadin is vulnerable to summer drought intensification. Agriculture, forest health, and wildfire risk are all affected.
3. **Intense precipitation and flooding**: The Inn valley's topography concentrates runoff. At 2°C warming, extreme rainfall events will be twice as probable, increasing flash flood and debris flow risk in tributary valleys (Val Roseg, Val Bernina, Val Morteratsch).
4. **Landslides and rockfall**: Permafrost degradation above 2,500 m increases rockfall frequency. The Val Müstair and Lower Engadin, with steep valley walls, are particularly exposed.
5. **Avalanches**: Shifting snow cover patterns create more complex avalanche conditions. Rain-on-snow events are becoming more common.

**DE**: Das Engadin steht vor einer spezifischen Mischung von Extremereignisrisiken:
1. **Hitzestress**: Weniger schwer als im Flachland, aber Rekordtemperaturen werden auch in der Höhe häufiger.
2. **Dürre**: Als bereits trockenes inneralpines Tal (700–1.000 mm/Jahr) ist das Engadin anfällig für verstärkte Sommerdürre.
3. **Starkniederschläge und Hochwasser**: Bei 2°C Erwärmung werden extreme Niederschlagsereignisse doppelt so wahrscheinlich.
4. **Murgänge und Steinschlag**: Permafrostdegradation über 2.500 m erhöht die Steinschlagfrequenz.
5. **Lawinen**: Veränderte Schneeverhältnisse schaffen komplexere Lawinenbedingungen.

**Cantonal timeline**: Graubünden must develop strategic risk planning for natural hazards by 2031 and implement protective measures by 2040.
