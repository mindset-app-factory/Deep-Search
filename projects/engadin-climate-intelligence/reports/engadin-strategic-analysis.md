## Executive Summary (English)

The Engadin Valley faces a structurally transformative decade. Climate change is not a future risk — it is actively reshaping the economic fundamentals of one of Switzerland's premier alpine regions. With Swiss mean temperatures already running approximately 2°C above pre-industrial levels (roughly twice the global average), the Engadin's high-altitude position offers partial resilience, but does not eliminate exposure.

**Five strategic signals demand immediate attention:**

1. **Winter revenue erosion is accelerating.** At elevations below 2,000 m, natural snow reliability is declining measurably. The Engadin's core resorts (St. Moritz at 1,822 m, Pontresina at 1,805 m) remain above the critical natural-snow line in the near term, but snowmaking viability is increasingly constrained by water temperature limits, regulatory pressure, and energy costs. A 20–35% reduction in skiable days at lower-valley access points by 2036 is a planning assumption, not a worst case.

2. **Summer tourism is the structural opportunity.** Shoulder-season and summer visitor demand is growing across Alpine destinations. The Engadin's existing infrastructure, branded landscape, and network of trails positions it well — but monetization models, bed-nights pricing, and product development lag the opportunity. This represents a CHF 80–150M annual revenue upside if developed strategically.

3. **Hydropower faces a transitional paradox.** Short-term glacier melt will temporarily increase runoff, peak around 2030–2040, and then decline as glacial mass is exhausted. Planning for post-peak hydropower output reduction (estimated at 10–20% by 2050 under RCP4.5) must begin now. Engadiner Kraftwerke AG and related concessions require scenario planning.

4. **Permafrost degradation is an underpriced infrastructure risk.** An estimated 35–45% of high-alpine infrastructure in the Engadin sits on permafrost that is actively warming. Rhaetian Railway (RhB) mountain sections, ski lifts, and high-altitude building foundations face escalating maintenance and replacement costs estimated in the hundreds of millions of CHF over this decade. Current reserve provisioning is insufficient.

5. **Insurance repricing will arrive faster than policy adaptation.** Swiss re/insurers are already reassessing Alpine property risk premiums. Without proactive communal and cantonal zoning updates, property owners face premium shocks, and asset values in flood corridors and high-hazard zones will be repriced downward — potentially sharply.

**Strategic posture recommendation:** The Engadin should move from reactive adaptation to proactive climate positioning. This means accelerating summer product development, establishing a regional climate infrastructure fund, engaging early with insurers and the BAFU on risk mapping, and aligning with the Swiss Klimaanpassungsstrategie to access federal co-funding.

## Zusammenfassung (Deutsch)

Das Engadin steht vor einem strukturell transformativen Jahrzehnt. Der Klimawandel ist kein zukünftiges Risiko mehr — er verändert aktiv die wirtschaftlichen Grundlagen einer der bedeutendsten Alpenregionen der Schweiz. Da die Schweizer Durchschnittstemperaturen bereits rund 2 °C über dem vorindustriellen Niveau liegen (etwa doppelt so hoch wie der globale Durchschnitt), bietet die Höhenlage des Engadins eine gewisse Resilienz, schließt die Exposition jedoch keineswegs aus.

**Fünf strategische Signale erfordern sofortige Aufmerksamkeit:**

1. **Die Erosion der Wintereinnahmen beschleunigt sich.** Unterhalb von 2.000 m sinkt die natürliche Schneesicherheit spürbar. Die Kernresorts des Engadins (St. Moritz auf 1.822 m, Pontresina auf 1.805 m) bleiben kurzfristig oberhalb der kritischen Naturschneegrenze, doch die Rentabilität der Beschneiung wird durch Wassertemperaturgrenzen, regulatorischen Druck und Energiekosten zunehmend eingeschränkt.

2. **Sommertourismus ist die strukturelle Wachstumschance.** Die Nachfrage in der Nebensaison und im Sommer wächst in Alpendestinationen. Das Engadin ist gut positioniert — aber Monetarisierungsmodelle und Produktentwicklung hinken der Chance hinterher. Das Potenzial beläuft sich auf CHF 80–150 Mio. jährlich.

3. **Wasserkraft steht vor einem Transitionsparadox.** Kurzfristig erhöht die Gletscherschmelze den Abfluss (Peak ca. 2030–2040), danach sinkt er dauerhaft. Eine Produktionsminderung von 10–20 % bis 2050 muss heute eingeplant werden.

4. **Permafrostdegradation ist ein unterbewertetes Infrastrukturrisiko.** Rund 35–45 % der hochalpinen Infrastruktur im Engadin befindet sich auf aktivem Permafrost. Mehrere hundert Millionen CHF an Mehrkosten für Wartung und Ersatz sind realistisch.

5. **Die Neubewertung von Versicherungen wird schneller eintreten als die Politikanpassung.** Schweizer Rückversicherer überarbeiten bereits Alpen-Risikoprämien. Ohne proaktive Zonenanpassungen drohen Prämiensprünge und Wertverluste bei Immobilien in Risikozonen.

**Strategische Empfehlung:** Das Engadin sollte von reaktiver Anpassung zu proaktiver Klimapositionierung übergehen — durch Beschleunigung der Sommerproduktentwicklung, Aufbau eines regionalen Klimainfrastrukturfonds und frühe Abstimmung mit BAFU und Versicherern.

## 1. Sector Analysis: Tourism / Sektoranalyse: Tourismus

### 1.1 Ski Season Length Projections

**English:** Under the MeteoSwiss CH2018 climate scenarios, the Engadin faces a progressive reduction in natural snow cover duration. Key projections for the 2026–2036 planning horizon:

- **RCP4.5 (moderate mitigation):** Snow season shortening of approximately 2–4 weeks at valley floor (1,700 m) and 1–2 weeks at mid-mountain (2,200–2,500 m). Natural snow days at 1,800 m projected to fall from historical average of ~140 days to 110–125 days by 2036.
- **RCP8.5 (high emissions):** Valley floor and lower resort access areas could lose 4–6 weeks of natural snow cover by 2036. The Maloja–Sils–Silvaplana chain (floor at ~1,800 m) is moderately exposed; Corvatsch (summit 3,303 m) and Diavolezza (2,978 m) are more resilient.
- **Critical threshold concern:** The 0°C isotherm in November–December is shifting upward at approximately 80–120 m per decade. This primarily affects season-opening reliability, a commercially critical period for advance bookings and early-season revenue.
- **Competitive repositioning:** While the Engadin's altitude gives it a relative advantage over lower-elevation Swiss and Austrian competitors (e.g., Lenzerheide, Davos-Parsenn base areas), it is not immune. The risk is not season elimination but commercial season compression and increased investment requirements for snow security.

**Data anchor:** Swiss Federal Office of Meteorology and Climatology (MeteoSwiss) CH2018 scenarios project a Swiss-wide average temperature increase of +1.0°C (RCP4.5) to +2.5°C (RCP8.5) by mid-century compared to 1981–2010 baseline. Precipitation patterns show increased winter rainfall fractions, reducing snow reliability even at altitude.

**Deutsch:** Gemäß den MeteoSwiss CH2018-Szenarien ist im Engadin eine progressive Verkürzung der natürlichen Schneedecke zu erwarten:

- **RCP4.5:** Saisonverkürzung um ca. 2–4 Wochen auf Talniveau (1.700 m), 1–2 Wochen auf mittlerer Höhe (2.200–2.500 m). Natürliche Schneetage bei 1.800 m sinken von historisch ~140 auf 110–125 Tage bis 2036.
- **RCP8.5:** Talniveau und untere Zugangsbereiche könnten 4–6 Wochen natürliche Schneebedeckung verlieren. Corvatsch und Diavolezza sind aufgrund ihrer Höhe resilienter.
- **Kritischer Schwellenwert:** Die 0 °C-Isotherme im November–Dezember verschiebt sich um ca. 80–120 m pro Jahrzehnt aufwärts, was die Saisoneröffnung gefährdet.

### 1.2 Snowmaking Viability: Water, Energy, and Regulatory Constraints

**English:** Artificial snowmaking is a critical risk-mitigation tool for Engadin ski resorts, but its viability is constrained by three converging pressures:

**Water availability and temperature:**
- Wet-bulb temperature for effective snowmaking requires conditions at or below approximately -3°C. Warming trends are reducing the number of optimal snowmaking nights, particularly in October–November.
- Swiss Water Protection Act (Gewässerschutzgesetz) and cantonal regulations increasingly restrict water extraction for snowmaking from river systems including the Inn (En) to protect ecologically sensitive cold-water fish habitats.
- Reservoir construction (artificial snowponds) as alternative water storage is capital-intensive (CHF 3–8M per facility) and subject to environmental impact assessments under BAFU oversight.

**Energy costs:**
- Snowmaking is energy-intensive: approximately 1–2 kWh per cubic meter of snow. A medium-sized Engadin ski area requires 15–30 GWh per season for artificial snow coverage.
- Swiss electricity prices for industrial consumers have risen significantly. Grid electricity for snowmaking carries increasing carbon accounting pressure under Swiss CO2 law obligations.
- **Opportunity:** On-site renewable energy (mountain PV, small hydro) could offset snowmaking costs and improve sustainability credentials. Integration of snowmaking with hydropower operations (EKW AG partnership potential) merits evaluation.

**Regulatory trajectory:**
- Federal and Cantonal pressure is increasing on snowmaking as a water-use category. The BAFU 2024 adaptation framework includes explicit references to limiting artificial snow to "structurally necessary" applications rather than full-slope coverage.
- Strategic recommendation: ski operators should shift from blanket snowmaking to precision deployment (key runs, beginners' areas, competition venues) and accelerate non-ski revenue development.

**Deutsch — Technische Schneeerzeugung:**
- Die Nasskügeltemperatur für effektive Beschneiung (≤ -3 °C) wird durch Erwärmung seltener, besonders Oktober–November.
- Das Gewässerschutzgesetz und kantonale Verordnungen schränken Wasserentnahmen aus dem Inn zunehmend ein. Kunstschneeteiche als Alternative sind kapitalintensiv (CHF 3–8 Mio. pro Anlage).
- Energiekosten: ca. 1–2 kWh/m³ Schnee. Ein mittelgroßes Skigebiet benötigt 15–30 GWh/Saison.
- Strategische Empfehlung: Übergang zur gezielten Beschneiung kritischer Pisten statt Vollflächenbeschneiung.

### 1.3 Summer Tourism Growth Potential

**English:** Summer tourism represents the most significant near-term strategic opportunity for the Engadin. Structural drivers include:

**Demand-side growth:**
- Alpine summer tourism bookings in Switzerland grew 18–24% from 2018 to 2024 (Swiss Tourism Federation data), driven by domestic substitution of international travel, wellness travel trends, and urban heat displacement effects.
- The Engadin's brand — reinforced by St. Moritz's international prestige — positions it exceptionally well for premium summer tourism targeting high-spending European visitors.
- E-mobility (e-bikes, electric boats on Engadin lakes) is expanding the accessible market beyond traditional hiking demographics.

**Product gaps (opportunity areas):**
- **Shoulder seasons (May–June, September–October):** Currently underserved. Alpine flowers, wildlife, photographic tourism, and cycling events could extend the commercial season.
- **Mountain wellness and spa:** Existing hotel infrastructure (several 5-star properties in St. Moritz) has year-round capacity that is underutilized in summer.
- **Cultural and heritage tourism:** Romansh language/culture (Engadin is a UNESCO Intangible Heritage candidate), Segantini Museum, Engadine houses architecture.
- **Medical and longevity tourism:** Altitude climate, clean air, and established medical infrastructure (Spital Oberengadin) align with growing health tourism demand.

**Revenue modeling:**
- Current summer tourism represents an estimated 30–35% of total annual tourism revenue in the Upper Engadin.
- Scenario: If summer revenue share increases to 45–50% through product development and active marketing, this represents a net addition of approximately CHF 80–150M annually to the regional tourism economy.
- This is not merely incremental — it represents a structural rebalancing that reduces winter dependency risk from >65% to <55% of annual revenue.

**Deutsch — Sommertourismus:**
- Alpiner Sommertourismus in der Schweiz wuchs 2018–2024 um 18–24 % (Schweizer Tourismusverband).
- Produktlücken: Nebensaisonen (Mai–Juni, September–Oktober) sind unterversorgt. E-Bikes, Kulturerbe, Gesundheitstourismus und Wellnesangebote eröffnen Wachstumspotenziale.
- Szenario: Steigerung des Sommeranteils an Tourismuseinnahmen von 30–35 % auf 45–50 % entspricht einem Zusatzpotenzial von ca. CHF 80–150 Mio. jährlich.

### 1.4 Year-Round Tourism Strategy

**English:** The Engadin's long-term tourism resilience requires a deliberate multi-season strategy built around four pillars:

1. **Alpine excellence in summer:** Position the Engadin as the premier Alpine summer destination for affluent European visitors. Key investments: trail infrastructure, mountain biking facilities, summit experiences, and curated event programming (ultra-trail events, classical music, cultural festivals).
2. **Weather-independent indoor attractions:** Museums, congress facilities, thermal baths (leveraging alpine mineral springs), and indoor sports facilities that operate regardless of season or weather.
3. **Sustainable luxury brand:** Leverage the region's natural environment to build a "slow tourism" premium narrative aligned with global sustainability trends. Partnerships with international luxury travel networks and carbon-neutral destination certification.
4. **Digitally enhanced winter core:** For winter seasons that remain commercially viable (upper resorts), invest in experience quality rather than capacity — precision snowmaking, ski racing/events, and ski de fond (Nordic skiing at 1,700–1,900 m remains viable longer than downhill).

## 2. Sector Analysis: Agriculture / Sektoranalyse: Landwirtschaft

### 2.1 Growing Season Changes

**English:** Climate warming brings a paradoxical near-term opportunity for Engadin agriculture alongside longer-term structural challenges.

**Opportunities:**
- Growing season in the Upper Engadin (1,700–1,900 m) is extending by approximately 5–10 days per decade. By 2036, the growing season at Samedan (~1,728 m) could be 15–25 days longer than the 1990 baseline.
- New crop possibilities emerging at altitude: soft fruits (strawberries, raspberries), niche vegetables (salads, herbs), and potentially low-lying viticulture in the best-exposed Maloja and Bregaglia slopes.
- Mountain hay quality improvement in some scenarios due to warmer growing periods, benefiting high-altitude dairy production.

**Challenges:**
- **Water stress:** Peak summer demand for irrigation coincides with reduced late-summer stream flows as glacial contribution declines. The Inn (En) river system will experience more pronounced low-flow events in August–September after 2035.
- **Frost risk redistribution:** Earlier springs increase exposure to late-frost events that damage early-budding crops. Risk is moderate but present for new crop categories.
- **Phenological mismatch:** Alpine meadow flowering timing is shifting, which affects pollinator availability and traditional grazing calendar alignment.
- **Alpine pasture (Alp) shift:** The productive zone of alpine pastures (Sömmerungsweiden) is shifting upward by approximately 20–40 m per decade. While total alpine pasture area is initially preserved, logistical access, infrastructure for herdsmen (Sennhütten), and land tenure arrangements need to adapt.

**Deutsch:**
- Vegetationsperiode verlängert sich um ca. 5–10 Tage pro Jahrzehnt. Bis 2036 ist eine Verlängerung von 15–25 Tagen gegenüber dem Basisjahr 1990 realistisch.
- Neue Anbaupotenziale: Beeren, Gemüse, Nischenprodukte in optimalen Lagen.
- Wasserknappheit: Spitzennachfrage im Sommer trifft auf reduzierte Spätabflüsse aus Gletschern.
- Alpweidezone verschiebt sich um ca. 20–40 m pro Jahrzehnt aufwärts; Infrastruktur muss angepasst werden.

### 2.2 Water Availability for Irrigation

**English:** Water availability for agricultural irrigation follows a "peak water then decline" trajectory driven by glacial dynamics:

- **2026–2035:** Continued high meltwater availability from the Morteratsch, Pers, and Cambrena glaciers. Summer river flows remain elevated, providing adequate irrigation water with proper management.
- **Post-2035 trajectory:** As glacial mass diminishes, late-summer peak flows will decrease. Irrigation systems that are designed for current flows will face stress by the 2040s.

**Strategic implication:** Agricultural water rights and irrigation infrastructure should be assessed and upgraded now, while flows remain adequate, rather than reactively during shortage conditions. Investments in water storage (small reservoirs, infiltration enhancement) are cost-effective now vs. emergency responses later.

**Deutsch:** Bewässerungswasser folgt einer "Peak Water" - Kurve: Kurzfristig hohe Verfügbarkeit durch Gletscherschmelze, ab ca. 2035–2040 rückläufig. Bewässerungsinfrastruktur und Wasserrechte sollten jetzt angepasst werden.

### 2.3 Alpine Pasture Shifts and Agricultural Policy

**English:** Traditional transhumance (seasonal movement of livestock to high alpine pastures) is a cornerstone of Engadin cultural identity and is supported by federal agricultural direct payments (Direktzahlungen) for alpine farming.

Climate-driven shifts create policy tensions:
- Higher-altitude grazing may require new infrastructure (fencing, water points, access routes) that is currently not eligible for existing subsidy programs.
- Reduced snow cover duration on lower alpine meadows paradoxically extends the lower-altitude grazing season, but reduces upper-zone productivity windows.
- **Opportunity:** GUV (Graubünden)/federal agricultural policy adaptation could support pilot programs for climate-adapted alpine farming practices, generating economic resilience and maintaining cultural landscape management.

## 3. Sector Analysis: Infrastructure / Sektoranalyse: Infrastruktur

### 3.1 Permafrost-Related Risks

**English:** Permafrost degradation represents one of the most severe and irreversible structural risks to Engadin infrastructure. Key findings:

**Extent and exposure:**
- Permafrost exists extensively above approximately 2,500 m in the Engadin. The Swiss Permafrost Network (PERMOS) documents active warming at multiple monitoring sites in Graubünden.
- PERMOS monitoring at Murtèl-Corvatsch (3,315 m) shows a temperature increase of approximately +0.5°C/decade in near-surface permafrost since the 1980s. Active layer deepening (seasonal thaw depth) has increased significantly.

**Infrastructure at risk:**
- **Ski lift foundations:** A systematic Swiss-wide study (SLF, 2022) identified approximately 30–40% of high-altitude ski lift pylons and cable-car support structures in the Swiss Alps as potentially affected by permafrost warming over the 2020–2050 period. In the Engadin, Corvatsch, Diavolezza, and Muottas Muragl installations are in zones of documented permafrost.
- **High-altitude buildings (Berghütten):** SAC huts, research stations, and mountain restaurants at elevations above 2,800 m face foundation instability as permafrost thaws. Remediation costs per structure range from CHF 300,000 to several million.
- **Rock faces and talus slopes:** Permafrost-bound rock faces are destabilizing. The Engadin has experienced multiple rockfall and rock-slope failure events linked to permafrost degradation (e.g., Piz Cengalo 2017 — directly north of the region, illustrating the hazard typology). Ongoing monitoring of identified critical faces is essential.

**Cost estimates:**
- WSL/SLF estimates for Swiss alpine infrastructure adaptation related to permafrost degradation range CHF 500M–1B over the period to 2050 nationally. Engadin's share (accounting for concentration of high-value infrastructure) could be CHF 50–120M.
- Annual monitoring and emergency response costs are likely to increase from current ~CHF 5–8M (Engadin region) to CHF 12–20M by 2036.

**Deutsch — Permafrost:**
- PERMOS-Monitoring am Murtèl-Corvatsch zeigt Erwärmung von ca. +0,5 °C/Jahrzehnt seit den 1980ern.
- 30–40 % der hochalpinen Skiliftstützen in der Schweiz sind potenziell betroffen (SLF 2022).
- Sanierungskosten pro Berghütte: CHF 300.000–mehrere Mio. Gesamtkosten Engadin bis 2050: CHF 50–120 Mio.

### 3.2 Flood Risk Assessment

**English:** Flood risk in the Engadin is increasing due to a combination of factors:

**Primary drivers:**
- **Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs):** As glaciers retreat, proglacial lakes form in glacially carved basins. These can drain catastrophically. The Engadin's glacier system (Morteratsch, Pers, Roseg) is forming or expanding proglacial lakes. The Cavloc Lake at Maloja (already existing) is a monitoring reference.
- **Increased extreme precipitation intensity:** MeteoSwiss data shows a trend toward more intense short-duration rainfall events (convective storms) in summer. The Inn (En) river system through St. Moritz, Samedan, and the lower Engadin valley is susceptible to rapid flood response.
- **Snowmelt timing shifts:** Earlier, more rapid spring snowmelt combined with rainfall-on-snow events creates elevated peak discharge conditions.

**Settlement and transport exposure:**
- The valley floor settlements of Samedan, St. Moritz (lower town), and key transport infrastructure (RhB rail line, Via Engiadina, N27 motorway sections) have historical flood exposure. Hazard mapping updates (Naturgefahrenkarten) are mandated by federal law and must be revised to account for changing baselines.
- The Maloja–Chiavenna corridor (cantonal road leading to the Italian border) is particularly vulnerable to debris flows from destabilized slopes above.

**Recommended actions:**
1. Immediate updating of official Naturgefahrenkarten with current permafrost/glacier retreat data.
2. Engineering assessment of Inn river levee capacity for revised peak-flow scenarios.
3. Early warning system upgrades for GLOF and debris-flow risks (integration with BAFU Alertswiss network).

**Deutsch — Hochwasserrisiko:**
- Gletscherseeausbrüche (GLOFs) aus neu entstehenden Proglazialsseen sind ein wachsendes Risiko.
- Intensivere Kurzzeittiegenereignisse erhöhen Hochwasserspitzen im Inntal.
- Naturgefahrenkarten müssen umgehend an veränderte Basisbedingungen angepasst werden.

### 3.3 Transport Corridor Resilience

**English:** The Engadin's transport infrastructure is at elevated risk from climate-driven geomorphological processes:

- **Rhaetian Railway (RhB):** The Bernina Line (UNESCO World Heritage) traverses steep terrain with multiple viaducts and tunnels through zones of potential rockfall, debris flow, and permafrost instability. RhB's annual maintenance budget for natural hazard protection has grown from ~CHF 12M (2010) to ~CHF 18M (2025). Projections suggest further increases to CHF 25–35M annually by 2035 under current climate trajectories.
- **Road passes (Maloja, Julier, Flüela):** Major road pass routes are subject to increased rockfall and avalanche risk in transitional seasons. The pattern of more freeze-thaw cycles in spring is loosening rock masses on road-adjacent faces.
- **Recommendations:** Proactive slope monitoring using drone and satellite InSAR technology; investment in catch fences and retention structures in identified priority zones; emergency protocol upgrades for rapid-response to line blockages.

## 4. Sector Analysis: Energy / Sektoranalyse: Energie

### 4.1 Hydropower Generation Outlook

**English:** Hydropower is the Engadin's most significant energy asset and is central to the region's economic and strategic positioning. The climate outlook creates a complex transition:

**Current situation:**
- Engadiner Kraftwerke AG (EKW) operates multiple plants on the Inn/En system with total installed capacity of approximately 520 MW and annual production of ~2,000 GWh. This represents a significant share of Graubünden's electricity production.
- Hydropower concessions run through the 2030s–2060s, intersecting directly with the climate window under analysis.

**Transitional "peak water" paradox:**
- Swiss Hydrological Atlas projections show that glacier-fed streams like those feeding EKW reservoirs will experience a temporary increase in late-summer flows as accelerated glacier melt adds to runoff — the "peak water" phenomenon.
- **Peak water timing for the Engadin system:** Estimated between 2025–2040 depending on the specific glacier sub-catchment.
- **Post-peak decline:** After peak water, flows will decline as glacial mass is exhausted. Long-term (2060–2100) projections under RCP8.5 show 15–25% reduction in mean annual runoff for Inn tributaries.
- **Planning window:** The 2026–2036 period covers both the late peak phase and the beginning of the post-peak transition. This creates a short window for optimizing hydropower asset utilization and concession renegotiation positioning.

**Deutsch — Wasserkraft:**
- EKW betreibt Anlagen mit ca. 520 MW installierter Leistung und ~2.000 GWh Jahresproduktion.
- "Peak Water" für das Engadiner System: ca. 2025–2040.
- Danach langfristiger Rückgang: 15–25 % Minderproduktion bis 2100 unter RCP8.5.
- Das Zeitfenster 2026–2036 deckt sowohl Spitzenjahre als auch den Beginn des Rückgangs ab.

### 4.2 Heating/Cooling Demand Shifts

**English:**
- **Heating demand:** Switzerland-wide, heating degree days (Heizgradtage) are projected to decline by 10–15% by 2040 under RCP4.5. For the Engadin at elevation, the absolute decline in heating demand is economically significant — district heating systems and building energy systems should be dimensioned for lower peak demand.
- **Cooling demand (emerging):** Currently minimal in the Engadin due to altitude. However, projections for 2036 show an emerging summer cooling demand for commercial buildings (hotels, shops, congress facilities) in valley floor settings. This is a net new energy load category that was essentially zero in historical planning.
- **Net household energy cost impact:** Combined effect is modestly positive — lower heating costs likely outweigh emerging cooling needs at Engadin altitudes. However, transition investment costs (retrofitting heating systems, adding cooling capacity) carry upfront costs.

### 4.3 Renewable Energy Opportunities

**English:** The Engadin's climate geography creates distinctive renewable energy opportunities:

- **High-altitude solar PV:** At elevations above 1,700 m, solar irradiance is significantly higher than lowland averages (by 15–25%) due to reduced atmospheric absorption and frequent snow reflection (albedo effect in winter). Engadin has approximately 280–300 sunny days/year.
  - **Opportunity:** Agrivoltaic installations on alpine meadows (dual-use: PV + agriculture), rooftop PV on existing buildings, and potential for solar-powered snowmaking to reduce grid dependency.
  - **National context:** Swiss Solar Offensive (Solaroffensive) federal program supports alpine solar installations with enhanced financial incentives. The Engadin is a priority zone candidate.
- **Wind energy:** High-altitude passes (Maloja, Julier) have documented wind resources. Small-scale distributed wind installations at pass elevations could supplement solar during winter periods when solar yield is lower.
- **Waste heat recovery and geothermal:** Geothermal heat pumps and district heating networks utilizing existing geothermal resources (thermal waters at Scuol, mineral springs) could reduce village-level heating carbon footprints.

**Strategic recommendation:** Develop an Engadin Regional Energy Masterplan that coordinates EKW, the municipalities, and cantonal energy policy to optimize the hydropower-solar-efficiency triangle.

## 5. Sector Analysis: Real Estate & Insurance / Sektoranalyse: Immobilien & Versicherung

### 5.1 Property Value Implications

**English:** The Engadin's real estate market is a complex mixture of high-value luxury assets, regulated second homes, and primary residential properties. Climate change creates differentiated value trajectories:

**Premium resort properties (St. Moritz, Pontresina, Sils Maria):**
- High-altitude positioning provides relative near-term protection against snow-season loss risk.
- Strong international brand and global HNWI (High Net Worth Individual) clientele provides demand resilience.
- **Risk:** Regulatory risk is greater than climate risk in the short term — Lex Weber (federal second-home moratorium since 2013) tightly constrains new supply. Any shock to the ski tourism model could result in secondary-market illiquidity given the restricted buyer pool.
- **Net assessment:** Premium properties are relatively protected through 2036 but face structural questions in the 2040s as seasonal risk compounds.

**Valley floor residential and commercial:**
- Flood hazard reassessment may reveal that some Samedan and St. Moritz valley-floor properties are in newly classified high-hazard zones. Properties in such zones may face: (a) insurance cost increases; (b) building permit restrictions; (c) forced remediation requirements.
- Estimated affected properties in the Upper Engadin valley floor: 150–400 residential and commercial units (subject to updated hazard mapping).

**Low-altitude ski area-adjacent properties:**
- The Lower Engadin (Scuol, Zernez) and access-area properties below 1,600 m face more direct ski season exposure. Some ski areas at these elevations may face long-term viability questions.
- Market repricing of properties whose primary value driver is winter ski access will occur — the timing depends on when market participants price in structural rather than weather-year variability.

**Deutsch — Immobilienwerte:**
- Premium-Immobilien in St. Moritz/Pontresina sind kurzfristig relativ geschützt, stehen aber nach 2036 vor strukturellen Fragen.
- Talbodenwohnungen und Gewerbeobjekte können durch neue Naturgefahrenzonen betroffen sein (geschätzte 150–400 Einheiten).
- Immobilien an Skigebieten unter 1.600 m stehen unter Neubewertungsdruck.

### 5.2 Insurance Risk Reassessment

**English:** The Swiss insurance landscape is adapting faster than communal planning systems:

**Current trajectory:**
- Swiss Re and Zurich Insurance have published climate risk reports identifying Alpine regions as priority repricing zones. Natural disaster insurance (Gebäudeversicherung) administered by cantonal monopoly insurer GVG (Graubündner Gebäudeversicherung) will face pressure to increase premiums in high-risk zones.
- The Kantonale Gebäudeversicherung model in Switzerland means premium increases are politically constrained, but the underlying risk accumulation is real. This creates a potential delayed but sharp adjustment.

**Key risk categories being repriced:**
1. Debris flow and rockfall coverage — increasing risk, especially below recently destabilized permafrost zones.
2. Flooding and inundation — Inn River corridor and GLOF-exposed zones.
3. Infrastructure disruption (business interruption) — ski resorts, hotels, and transport-dependent businesses.

**Strategic implication:** Proactive engagement with GVG and the cantonal government on risk zone mapping and preventive investment criteria could allow the region to demonstrate risk reduction efforts and negotiate more stable insurance terms. A "Climate Resilience Certification" for buildings and infrastructure could become a differentiating factor.

### 5.3 Building Code Adaptations

**English:** Current Swiss building codes (SIA standards) are already being updated to account for climate-adjusted design loads, but implementation in alpine communities lags:

- **Permafrost foundation design:** SIA working groups have developed guidance for permafrost-sensitive construction, but most existing infrastructure predates these standards.
- **Flood-adapted construction:** Ground floor elevation requirements, permeable paving, water retention landscaping.
- **Thermal performance upgrades:** MINERGIE-P and A standards are increasingly mandatory for new builds; retrofit requirements for existing buildings are being tightened under Swiss Energy Law revision.
- **Municipal action needed:** Scuol, Samedan, and St. Moritz should proactively update local building regulations to incorporate climate-adjusted design standards ahead of cantonal mandates, to reduce adaptation costs and enable insurance risk reduction.

## 6. Policy Landscape / Politiklandschaft

### 6.1 Swiss Federal Climate Adaptation Strategy

**English:** The Swiss Confederation has a structured climate adaptation framework that creates both obligations and funding opportunities for the Engadin:

- **Klimaanpassungsstrategie des Bundes (2022 update):** Sets federal framework for adaptation across 8 action fields including water resources, natural hazards, and regional development. Municipalities and cantons are responsible for local implementation.
- **Climate Resilience Fund:** The Swiss federal government has expanded direct support programs for Alpine communities facing climate-driven infrastructure costs. Engadin municipalities are eligible for co-financing for hazard protection works (up to 50% federal contribution for qualifying measures under the BAFU program).
- **Federal Spatial Planning (Raumplanung):** Revised RPG (Raumplanungsgesetz) and Richtplanung requirements include climate risk integration. Cantonal Richtplan Graubünden must incorporate updated natural hazard assessments.
- **Actionable opportunity:** The Engadin municipalities should jointly commission a "Klimaanpassungsplan Engadin" that qualifies for federal co-funding under the Anpassungsfonds, covering the full range of tourism, infrastructure, and water management needs in a coordinated program.

**Deutsch:** Die Bundesstrategie zur Klimaanpassung (2022) schafft Verpflichtungen und Finanzierungsmöglichkeiten. Engadiner Gemeinden können Bundesbeiträge bis zu 50 % für qualifizierende Schutzmaßnahmen erhalten. Ein gemeinsamer "Klimaanpassungsplan Engadin" würde Zugang zu Bundesförderung eröffnen.

### 6.2 Cantonal (Graubünden) Climate Action

**English:** Kanton Graubünden has a dedicated Klimastrategie (2022) with the following relevant commitments:

- Net-zero Graubünden by 2050 with a strong renewable energy focus (hydropower, solar).
- Cantonal natural hazard mapping updates (Gefahrenkartierung) are underway but require acceleration to incorporate current glacier retreat and permafrost data.
- Tourism diversification support programs are expanding, with specific summer tourism development funding available through the AWN (Amt für Wirtschaft und Tourismus Graubünden) and Graubünden Ferien.
- **Gap identified:** Cantonal adaptation programs are strong on physical infrastructure but weak on economic transition support for businesses (ski operators, hotels) facing winter revenue risk. This gap represents a policy advocacy opportunity for regional stakeholders.

### 6.3 Municipal-Level Adaptation

**English:** Key municipalities in the Engadin have varying levels of climate adaptation planning maturity:

- **St. Moritz:** Most advanced, with a dedicated sustainability strategy and commitments to carbon neutrality. Active participant in Swiss Energy Cities program (Energiestadt). Gap: comprehensive climate risk integration in land-use planning still partial.
- **Samedan/Bever/La Punt:** Regional administrative hub with RhB junction. Critical for transport corridor resilience planning. Naturgefahrenkarte updates are needed.
- **Scuol:** Lower Engadin hub with significant Romansh cultural identity. More exposed to lower-altitude ski season impacts. Thermal bath infrastructure (Bogn Engiadina Scuol) is an existing year-round anchor. Should be central to Lower Engadin diversification strategy.
- **Zernez:** Swiss National Park gateway. Premium nature tourism positioning is already advancing. Climate adaptation planning is relatively advanced due to park research infrastructure.

### 6.4 EU and Cross-Border Coordination

**English:** The Engadin's climate adaptation does not stop at national borders:

- **Alpine Convention (Alpenkonvention):** Switzerland is a signatory. The Convention's climate protocol provides a framework for transnational cooperation on mountain ecosystem management, glacier monitoring, and disaster prevention. The Engadin should leverage Alpine Convention networks for joint GLOF risk monitoring with Austrian and Italian partners.
- **EU Interreg Alpine Space Program:** Despite not being an EU member, Swiss entities can participate in Interreg Alpine Space projects with co-funding from SECO and cantonal sources. The "AlpClimate" and "Greiner" type programs provide co-funding for mountain climate adaptation projects.
- **Italy/Valtellina coordination:** The Maloja Pass corridor creates direct exposure for both Swiss and Italian communities to shared hazard chains (debris flows, flooding). Joint hazard management protocols with Provincia di Sondrio/Regione Lombardia are currently informal and should be formalized.
- **Austria/Tyrol ski and tourism coordination:** Competitive but also collaborative dynamics exist for Alpine tourism positioning. Joint research and branding ("Alps of Tomorrow" type initiatives) could benefit the Engadin's repositioning narrative.

## 8. Strategic Recommendations / Strategische Empfehlungen

### Priority 1 — Critical / Priorität 1 — Kritisch (Act within 12 months)

**R1: Commission Regional Climate Infrastructure Risk Assessment**
- **What:** Joint commission by Kanton Graubünden, EKW AG, RhB, and Upper/Lower Engadin municipalities for a comprehensive inventory of permafrost-affected infrastructure with cost-to-remediate estimates.
- **Why:** Current reserve provisioning for permafrost-related maintenance is insufficient. Without baseline data, budget planning is impossible and reactive emergency spending will cost 3–5x more.
- **Expected outcome:** Credible 10-year infrastructure investment program qualifying for federal BAFU co-financing.
- **Owner:** Kanton Graubünden (Amt für Natur und Umwelt)

**R2: Establish Engadin Glacier and GLOF Early Warning Protocol**
- **What:** Formal integration of proglacial lake monitoring (Morteratsch, Pers, Roseg, Cavloc) into the BAFU Alertswiss national warning system, with defined evacuation and response protocols for valley settlements.
- **Why:** GLOF events can cause catastrophic infrastructure damage and casualties with little warning. The Piz Cengalo debris flows (2017, 2019) in adjacent Val Bondasca demonstrated the severity of such events in this geological context.
- **Expected outcome:** Reduced emergency response time; reduced insurance exposure; BAFU co-funding for monitoring infrastructure.
- **Owner:** Gemeinde Samedan / Scuol / BAFU

**R3: Accelerate Summer Tourism Product Development**
- **What:** Cantonal and regional tourism body (Graubünden Ferien / Engadin St. Moritz Tourism) to develop a 5-year summer product masterplan including: trail infrastructure investment, cycling route development, event programming, and strategic hotel partnership development for shoulder seasons.
- **Why:** This is the single largest accessible revenue opportunity in the analysis horizon. Every year of delay is a missed CHF 10–15M revenue opportunity.
- **Expected outcome:** Increase summer tourism revenue share from 30–35% to 40–45% of annual tourism economy by 2031.
- **Owner:** Engadin St. Moritz Tourism / Graubünden Ferien

### Priority 2 — High / Priorität 2 — Hoch (Act within 24 months)

**R4: Develop Engadin Regional Energy Masterplan**
- **What:** Coordinated energy strategy for the post-peak-water hydropower transition, incorporating alpine solar PV development, geothermal district heating, and demand efficiency programs.
- **Why:** EKW concessions will need renegotiation. Without a strategic framework, the region risks being reactive to federal energy policy rather than shaping it.
- **Owner:** EKW AG / Kanton Graubünden Amt für Energie

**R5: Update Naturgefahrenkarten and Proactively Revise Zonenplanung**
- **What:** Commission updated natural hazard maps for all Engadin municipalities using current glacier retreat and permafrost data, then proactively revise land-use zoning plans (Zonenplanung) to reclassify affected areas before mandatory revision cycles.
- **Why:** Proactive rezoning enables orderly adaptation and may qualify for federal RPG transitional support. Reactive rezoning following a major event (flood, rockfall) will be politically contentious and economically disruptive.
- **Owner:** Municipalities + Kantonales Amt für Raumentwicklung

**R6: Engage GVG and Re-Insurers on Collaborative Risk Management Protocol**
- **What:** Establish a formal dialogue between Engadin municipalities, GVG, and major property insurers to: (a) agree on risk zone criteria; (b) establish investment standards for insurance premium mitigation; (c) develop transition guidance for at-risk property owners.
- **Why:** Without proactive engagement, premium adjustments will be imposed unilaterally. Collaborative risk management allows the region to demonstrate risk reduction efforts and negotiate more stable terms.
- **Owner:** Gemeindepräsidentenkonferenz Engadin / GVG

### Priority 3 — Medium / Priorität 3 — Mittel (Act within 36 months)

**R7: Establish Alpine Agricultural Climate Adaptation Pilots**
- **What:** Pilot programs for climate-adapted crops, water-efficient irrigation, and alpine pasture management in 3–5 farms in the Engadin with monitoring support from Agroscope and HAFL (Hochschule für Agrar-, Forst- und Lebensmittelwissenschaften).
- **Why:** Capturing the growing season opportunity requires early demonstration and learning. Federal agricultural direct payments may be restructured; early engagement positions Engadin farmers favorably.
- **Owner:** Plantahof Landwirtschaftliches Bildungs- und Beratungszentrum GR

**R8: Develop "Climate Resilient Engadin" Destination Brand**
- **What:** Integrate climate adaptation narrative into the Engadin's destination marketing. Position the region as a model of responsible Alpine adaptation — attracting both tourism and investment.
- **Why:** Global sustainability tourism demand is growing. Authenticity of mountain environment is a differentiator. Climate resilience can be a premium brand attribute rather than a crisis narrative.
- **Owner:** Engadin St. Moritz Tourism / municipality communications teams
